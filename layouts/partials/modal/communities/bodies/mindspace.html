Description

I joined Mindspace at its founding in late 2014 as a Board Member, drawn to a mission I believed was structurally underserved: bridging the gap between academic environments and the real market. Most university-born ideas die inside institutions not because they lack merit, but because the people behind them have no systematic pathway to the outside world. During the 2014 to 2017 period that I was actively involved, Mindspace was built to be that pathway.

My early work centered on two things that I believed would determine whether Mindspace actually mattered beyond the university campus: curriculum and community. I initiated and organized the Y Combinator "How to Start a Startup" sessions at NTUA, running for 20 weeks with an average of 15 participants per session, one of the first times that kind of structured, practitioner-led startup content was brought systematically into a Greek academic setting. The PR and outreach work consumed most of my bandwidth during those first two years, deliberately so. Building a genuine connection between the organization and the broader Athens startup ecosystem required showing up constantly, initiating relationships, and being present at every relevant conversation happening in the city.

Most of the programs, events, and activities Mindspace ran during that period started as ideas that I pushed forward and helped operationalize. On the structural side, I advocated strongly for moving away from the large, slow-moving silos the organization had started with, toward smaller, flexible, high-velocity teams. The then-Chairman embraced that direction and carried it forward. The AI Club I founded during the same period was a shorter experiment, active for only a few months, but it reflected the same instinct: that the organization should be tracking what was coming, not just what was already established.

By 2016 I had moved into the Chairman role, which meant taking ownership of the full operation across two city chapters, Athens and Patras, with a team of 40 members and a community of 80+ pre-startup tech teams. The work shifted from building internal systems to sustaining them at scale while managing external relationships. We secured over €50,000 in funding from sponsors, deepened our existing collaboration with the U.S. Embassy, which began a year earlier through joint activities and evolved into formal sponsorship, while also initiating a new cross-border innovation partnership with the French Embassy. We also launched "Startup Secrets", a YouTube series of founder conversations that reached 25,000+ views and that I genuinely believed could become the resource every university student in Greece never had access to. Being featured in Kathimerini, Greece's leading newspaper, during this period was a signal that the work had reached a level of visibility that extended well beyond the university walls we started in.
